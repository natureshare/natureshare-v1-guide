Glossary

Terms Used in NatureShare
 (this page is under development)
 aff.  'with affinities to' - eg. "Caladenia aff. catenata (South Gippsland)". Most often used as 'sp. aff.', as in "Dianella sp. aff. longifolia (Benambra)", meaning a probable new species of Dianella that has affinities with Dianella longifolia, but is different to Dianella longifolia. The 'aff.' and 'sp. aff.' are used for plants that haven't been formally named yet, or for entities that haven't been fully studied yet. When the proper research is completed and published in a scientific journal the 'aff.' and 'sp. aff.' names are either discarded or, more usually, they are named as new species.
 attribute attributes are important attributes of a particular species (also called biological attributes). The format for attributes is "Attribute: Descriptor" (eg. 'flower: yellow', 'leaf: prickly', 'wing: green') which is usually in the form "Noun: Adjective". Attributes are ONLY relevant to the Species Section and Search Section.
 biological attribute
 biological attributes are important attributes of a particular species. The format for biological attributes is "Attribute: Descriptor" (eg. 'flower: yellow', 'leaf: prickly', 'wing: green') which is usually in the form "Noun: Adjective". Biological Attributes are ONLY relevant to the Species Section and Search Section.
 category categories are groups of species sharing common attributes that are recognised by a commonly used name; eg. 'parrot', 'wattle', 'butterfly', 'weed', 'rare_threatened', 'fern', 'fauna_introduced', 'extinct_vic', 'EPBC-listed', etc. Categories are ONLY relevant to the the Species Section and Search Section.
 c.f. confer - meaning "compare" or "consult".
 classification formal/scientific classifications; eg. the two main ones are arguably 'family' and 'order'. These take the form "family: orchidaceae", "order: lepidoptera", etc. Classifications are ONLY relevant to the Species Section and Search Section.
 s.l. sensu lato - 'in the broad sense' - means the species name as it was known prior to a recent split of the name into more than one species. For example, until recently "Alternanthera denticulata" was well known but it has recently undergone a split into two species, the name Alternanthera denticulata was retained (with a reduced distribution) and it is known post-split as "Alternanthera denticulata s.s." and the new species split from Alternanthera denticulata is "Alternanthera sp. 1 (Plains)". These two new species (Alternanthera denticulata s.s. and Alternanthera denticulata sp. 1) were both previously included in what is now called "Alternanthera denticulata s.l.". If you don't know about the split it is safest to use the s.l. name, then do some research to find out which species it is now, and change the name on the observation/collection when you know. In time, NatureShare will document these changes well - as it does now for Alternanthera denticulata via the 'Similar Species' system and through inclusion of informative 'Biological Attributes'. We need your help to add 'Similar Species' and 'Biological Attributes' content to NatureShare as you come across it (especially if you know about the split intimately). cf. s.s..
 s.s. sensu stricto - 'in the strict sense' - means the species name as known after a recent split of the name into more than one species. See the entry under "s.l. for a full explanation. If you don't know about the split it is safest to use the s.l. name, then do some research to find out which species it is now, and change the name on the observation/collection when you know. 
 sensu 'in the sense of' - eg. "Viola hederacea sensu Thiele & Prober" refers to Viola hederacea as described by Thiele & Prober. This outdates "Viola hederacea sensu Entwisle" and all others. You should assign 'sensu Thiele & Prober' if you are familiar with the Thiele & Prober split of Viola hederacea (which includes Viola eminens, etc) and assign 'sensu Entwisle' if you are not familiar with Thiele & Prober's work. Assign 'sensu Entwisle' if you are not sure.
 sp. species, singular.
 sp. aff.
 'species with affinities to' - eg. "Dianella sp. aff. longifolia (Benambra)", meaning a probable new species of Dianella that has affinities with Dianella longifolia, but is different to Dianella longifolia. The 'aff.' and 'sp. aff.' are used for plants that haven't been formally named yet, or for entities that haven't been fully studied yet. When the proper research is completed and published in a scientific journal the 'aff.' and 'sp. aff.' names are either discarded or, more usually, they are named as new species.
 spp. species, plural.
 spp. agg.
 'species (plural) aggregated' - a combination of two or more species. Most often used for difficult combinations of species as in "Rubus fruticosus spp. agg." (Blackberry) of which there are several species, and it is sometimes easier just to know that it is one of those species (without specifying which one, which can be very difficult). Other examples include "Convolvulus erubescens spp. agg." which also represents a cluster of similar species.
 spreading 90 degrees; as in stem hairs 90 degrees to the stem.
 subsp. subspecies, singular - a taxonomic category below species rank. A naturally occurring subdivision of a species that exhibits small, but consistent, morphological variations from other subdivisions of the same species, and so much so that it is distinct enough to warrant its own name. Different subspecies are usually geographically isolated.
 subspp. subspecies, plural.
 tag any important feature of the observation/photo (eg. flower, male, leaf). Tags are ONLY relevant to the Observations Section.
 var. varietas or variety - a taxonomic category below species rank. A naturally occurring subdivision of a species that exhibits small, but consistent, morphological variations from other subdivisions of the same species, and so much so that it is distinct enough to warrant its own name. Different varieties are not necessary geographically isolated.
 X a hybrid - if X occurs between two species names it is a hybrid between those two species (eg. Acacia aspera X pycnantha is a hybrid between Acacia aspera and Acacia pycnantha). If X occurs at the start of a name it is a hybrid between two genera and the following name is a combination of the two genera (eg. X Glossadenia spp. is a hybrid between a Glossodia spp. and a Caladenia spp.).
