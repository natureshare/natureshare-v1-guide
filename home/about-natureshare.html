About NatureShare

NatureShare is a database for individuals and groups to document and share information about their local environment across Victoria, Australia.

Everything you put into NatureShare (photos, locations, biological attributes) is shared in a real and functional way with everyone across Victoria.

NatureShare is free to everyone and can be used by anyone (individuals, groups, councils, companies, etc). Anyone, or any group, can set up species 'collections' for their own property, for parks/reserves, for areas/towns, for anything, anywhere (currently for Victoria only but it is hoped this will be extended further in the near future).

You can easily integrate NatureShare into your existing website and you can even access the NatureShare code and write your own code to help improve NatureShare. If we all share, we all benefit.

NatureShare is for documenting observations of remnant & self-sustaining flora/fauna (ie. plants, weeds, animals, introduced animals, insects, anything, but not plants in revegetation sites, garden plants, pets, etc).

Everyone enjoys finding a new bird or plant or animal on their property and in the bush, and everyone likes to share what they have seen. NatureShare allows us to share our observations.

How does the sharing part of NatureShare work?
  1. Your photos are shared across collections; eg. if a new species list in a new 'Collection A' is generated (e.g. from the web or from a paper-based list you've been developing), photos from other collections are automatically shared with the new collection, generating the beginnings of a photo-based e-book for your collection instantaneously. Then, as new photos are collected at the site of Collection A, those new photos take precedence over shared photos.
  2. Once you enter 'biological attributes' into NatureShare, they are shared for all to use. This means, if biological attributes are already entered in NatureShare prior to Collection A being established the new collection becomes immediately searchable. Most importantly, once attributes are entered, they are in NatureShare forever and there is no need for individuals and groups to 'reinvent the wheel' on their existing website.
  3. NatureShare makes photos and other information available for anyone who wants to use them (eg. for books, newsletters, articles) so long as proper credit is given under a Creative Commons attribution scheme.
  4. NatureShare makes it possible to share local knowledge with the local community, and to share it with visitors to your area. And vice-versa, people living in your area and visitors to an area can add to local collections if they see something of interest.
  5. Many individuals and groups (e.g. birdos, plant groups) regularly go on excursions/walks and some make formal or informal lists and photos of what they see. All of this information can be shared for the benefit of all on NatureShare.
  6. All the existing features in NatureShare are shared for anyone to use. There is no need for individuals and groups to keep 're-inventing the wheel'. NatureShare is limited only by the imagination of users - see picture right for an example of the amazing things that people have added to NatureShare (Caladenia congesta).
  7. If you think of a new feature for NatureShare you can build it yourself (or apply for grants to pay for someone to build it for you). Once you've built it, you share it with others so we (and Nature) can all benefit from your goodwill. NatureShare is an OpenGL web 2.0 application. This means code can be made available to people/groups if you want to help develop NatureShare further.
NatureShare's History

NatureShare is a project conceived, designed and implemented by Reilly Beacom and Russell Best. It was launched in 2011.

The initial focus of NatureShare was the Keilor-Werribee plains (north & west of Melbourne), Riddells Creek and the Macedon Range thanks to funding and in-kind support from people and groups in this area. On its formal launch in August 2011, flora and fauna information for the Keilor-Werribee Plains, Riddells Creek and the Macedon Range was reasonably well developed and the project was then opened up across Victoria.

The project was funded by Riddells Creek Landcare, APS Keilor Plains and DSE's Vision for Werribee Plains project, with the addition of an extraordinary amount of volunteer and in-kind support from people and groups in Riddells Creek, the Macedon Range, across the Keilor-Werribee plains and beyond.

NatureShare is a free, web-based tool for individuals, groups and communities to upload observations, sightings, photos & species information, and everyone shares the knowledge across Victoria.

NatureShare currently includes in its database all the plants, all the major animals groups and many insect groups known to occur in Victoria (currently over 16,000 species names) with many more to be added shortly (including more insect & invertebrate groups, fish and much more).

What was the motivation behind the creation of NatureShare?
  • Many individuals and groups across Victoria have enormous amounts of knowledge and display amazing generosity in sharing their knowledge. This knowledge, if combined, is a staggering prospect. Much of this knowledge is often not shared effectively and, worse still, sometimes becomes lost as key people leave groups/areas for whatever reason. The authors of NatureShare are in groups whose members have an enormous combined knowledge. NatureShare allows us to share our knowledge, not only between ourselves but across our local communities and beyond. NatureShare is designed so anyone can do the same.
  • Many individuals and groups observe and sometimes collect valuable information, go on field trips, conduct informal surveys. All of this information has wider use ... and with NatureShare there is somewhere to formally document, share and gather this information in a meaningful way.
  • The best information is local information. There are many 'top-down' databases around that are good in giving a State-wide overview but these often have limited use locally. There are no 'bottom-up' databases around that build State-wide information from the local level, where it is needed and used the most.
  • One problem with traditional websites is the need to continually 're-invent the wheel'. NatureShare was built not to replace existing websites but to help those who want to add features without 're-inventing the wheel' and so we can all share each other's information. NatureShare can be used as a stand-alone website for individuals or groups who have not got their own website but it is designed to be integrated into existing websites, blogs, emails, etc.
  • Nature belongs to us all and, frankly, it is under pressure in many areas. Before NatureShare, there was no generally available tool that individuals and groups could use to effectively document what species live in their neighbourhood. This tool is now available.
Web 2.0 technology has made NatureShare possible. Wikipedia definition: "A Web 2.0 site allows users to interact and collaborate with each other as consumers of user-generated content in a virtual community, in contrast to websites where users are limited to the active viewing of content that they created and controlled." NatureShare is built using shared code, using freely available systems and is designed primarily to be used using freely available systems (eg. Mozilla Firefox, Google Chrome and Safari). In turn, NatureShare source code will be made available under a GPL sharing licence.
